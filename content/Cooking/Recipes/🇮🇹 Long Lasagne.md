_This Lasagne recipe takes quite some time to prepare (nearly 8 hours!), but most of the time is spent allowing the flavours to meld together, so it's not all cooking. Best prepared the day before, then cooked just before you're ready to eat!_

> [!warning]
> Always wash your hands after handling raw meat, along with any surfaces it touches - unless you enjoy food poisoning.

> [!info]
> This recipe requires a pan / skillet that is safe to go in the oven for an extended period.
> **Do not use pans with (non-detachable) plastic handles, or cast iron (the acid in the tomato may ruin it!).**
> You will also need a lasagne tray or deep dish baking tray.


# Ingredients
## For the Ragu
* Olive oil
- 900g Beef Mince
	- Whatever fat content you like.
- 2 medium to large onions
- 2 garlic cloves
- 2 _level_ tablespoons Plain Flour
- 150ml beef stock
	- You can either buy ready made packs of this, or the simplest way is to use a crumbled oxo cube in 190ml of boiling water (and discard the rest).
- 1 teaspoon Caster sugar
- 3 tablespoons Tomato purée
- 1 tablespoon Chopped Thyme
	- If you don't make lots of recipes using fresh herbs, I can recommend buying frozen herbs from a supermarket, then simply leaving the quantity you need out for a half hour before cooking - it keeps forever so long as the bag's properly resealed.
- 2x 400g tins Chopped Tomatoes
## For the White Sauce
- 50g Butter
- 50g Plain Flour
- 750ml **hot** milk
	- Either heat on the stove to a simmer, or in the microwave.
	- Hot means **hot**, not warm.
- 2 teaspoons Dijon mustard
- 50g Parmesan Cheese, finely grated
	- Usually you can buy this as a wedge in the cheese section.
	- I wouldn't recommend the ready-grated stuff for this recipe.
- salt and pepper
## You'll also need
- 12 lasagne sheets
	- I find best results with the fresh kind - dried can be a pain to work with.
	- If using fresh, be sure to read the instructions on the back of the packet for re-hydrating before using.
- 75g grated mature cheddar cheese
	- Pre-grated is fine.
# Instructions

>[!important] Preheat your oven to 160°c conventional (140°c fan, Gas Mk.3)
## Ragu
1. Heat a large skillet / frying pan until hot with ~2 tablespoons olive oil, then fry the mince medium-high until browned. Remove once cooked, and transfer to a plate.
	* While waiting for the mince to brown, consider doing Step 2.
2. Roughly chop the two onions, and _smash_ the garlic cloves[^1], following up with a rough chop.
3. Add the onions and garlic to the pan on a medium heat, add a little more olive oil (around a tablespoon) if necessary, then gently cook until the onions soften up, stirring occasionally so that nothing burns.
4. Return the mince to the pan, and stir in the two _level_ tablespoons of flour, which should produce a much dryer mix. Add the stock and allow it to heat up to a simmer / boil as you stir it in, then add the teaspoon of caster sugar, 3 tablespoons of tomato purée, and 1 tablespoon of Thyme. Stir until everything is well mixed.
5. Add the two cans of chopped tomatoes, stir, and bring back to the boil. Once boiling, take off the heat, cover the pan, and put everything in the oven for **1.5 hours.**
	* Covering the pan can be a bit of a tricky one, especially if your lid has plastic on it. I usually opt to tightly wrap the pan in one or two pieces of kitchen (aluminium) foil, leaving as little gap as possible.

>[!tip]
>The white sauce can be made in around 5-10 minutes, so feel free to tidy up and stick your feet up for a while.
## White Sauce
1. Melt the 50g of butter in a saucepan over a medium heat, then add the 50g plain flour. Whisk until you get a thick paste after around a minute[^2]. 
2. **Slowly and gently** add the **hot** milk bit by bit, constantly whisking until all milk is incorporated.
	* I can't emphasise **slowly** strongly enough. Add a bit until the mix goes dry (it often likes to stick inside the whisk), then add a bit more, etcetera. If you add too much, it'll go lumpy, and then you'll have your work cut out with the whisk trying to get it to re-incorporate.
3. Continue heating the sauce while whisking until it's thickened to something resembling ketchup, then turn the heat off. Add the 2 teaspoons of Dijon Mustard and 50g of grated Parmesan Cheese, a good amount of salt and pepper, and stir well until everything's nicely incorporated.

## Lasagne, Assemble
>[!tip] Assembly Tips
>* The amount of ragu and white sauce you use on each layer will depend on how many layers you can achieve. Make a plan before you start blindly ladling to keep things as even as possible.
>* If you want to freeze your unprepared lasagne as portions, _line the dish with baking paper or kitchen foil_ with enough overhang to cover the top.

1. Ladle a serving of your ragu into the bottom of a ~2.5L shallow ovenproof dish, and spread around so it's a nice even layer. Spoon a serving of the white sauce over the top of this, then add a bit of salt and pepper. Arrange one layer of your lasagne sheets of choice over the top, trying to leave as little gap as possible.
2. Now repeat Step 1 over and over until you've used your last sheets, then add your remaining ragu and white sauce on top. Sprinkle the grated cheddar gratuitously over the top, then tightly wrap in kitchen foil (doing your best to leave an air gap on top!).
3. **Refrigerate for at least 6 hours** to allow the pasta to soften and the flavours to get to know each other.
>[!question]- Freezing your lasagne?
> Freeze the lasagne after it's finished refrigerating, otherwise the flavours won't properly meld.
> 
> You might also want to consider portioning out your lasagne after refrigerating but before freezing, to make it nice and easy to heat up later on down the line.

## It's Pasta Time
>[!important] Preheat your oven to 200°c conventional (180°c fan, Gas Mk.6)
1. Cook in the pre-heated oven _with foil_ for 30 minutes.
2. After 30 minutes, carefully *remove the foil* and allow to cook uncovered for another 15 minutes, or until it looks super delicious.

>[!question]- Cooking from frozen?
> If cooking Frozen Lasagne, double these times (with your own judgement).

**Ideally leave 30 minutes before serving**, otherwise you'll find you're trying to serve wet slop. The extra bit of cooling time afterwards helps you serve nice big cubes of lasagne-e goodness.

[^1]: Smashing a garlic clove means to remove its root, then push it against a chopping board with the flat of a large knife, like you're smearing it. You'll end up with something a few millimetres thick.
[^2]: Congratulations, you have made a _Beurre Manié_!
