# 💸 Money, and the spending thereof
* Transit
	* Just use Suica on iOS
	* Topup via Monzo / Apple Pay for fee-free
	* **This is not a cash hole** / cannot be turned into cash (but read below)
* Exchanging eMoney for Goods and Services
	* Most stores take Suica / IC, especially conbinis / restaurants / arcades / major shopping centres
		* To give you some idea: https://www.jreast.co.jp/e/suicamoney/shop.html
	* So do some vending machines, especially those near stations
	* Some stores do EMV, though I have no idea how prevalent this is now
		* Especially Amex acceptance (ignoring the obscene foreign transaction fee)
	* **Always be prepared to pay cash if unsure**
* Exchanging eMoney for **e**Goods and **e**Services
	* **Basically not happening, you need a Japanese card and address.**

* Cash, and the exchange thereof
	* The old trick used to be Monzo at 7Bank / post office, but apparently there’s a substantial usage fee now
	* Pulling cash and putting it into Suica is silly now because of Apple Pay topups, but can be useful for loose change above ¥10
* Tax exemption
	* Applicable if you spend more than 10,000 JPY in a single transaction at most larger stores
	* You’ll need to show your passport and get the receipt stapled into it, but you’ll get consumption tax taken off your bill
	* Receipts must be shown at outbound customs otherwise immigration won’t let you out (yes that’s a thing there)

# 🚨 Emergency Preparedness
* Remember, Japan does occasionally decide to have a bit of a wobbly one
* All fingers crossed you won’t get caught up in a major one, but it’s sensible to be prepared in case you are
* The general rule of thumb is that anything below a Magnitude 4 is barely noticeable
* For awareness:
	* Ensure iOS Emergency Alerts are **fully** enabled once you’ve landed and have a Japanese (e)Sim installed
	* For earthquake alerts / further information on whether the thing you just felt was actually a quake or just being a bit drunk, use Yurekuru Call: https://apps.apple.com/gb/app/%E3%82%86%E3%82%8C%E3%81%8F%E3%82%8B%E3%82%B3%E3%83%BC%E3%83%AB/id1560592402

		* You may need to fine-tune the settings so it doesn’t yell at you every thirty seconds about a magnitude 1.5
	* NHK World’s app provides emergency information as notifications: https://apps.apple.com/gb/app/nhk-world-japan/id350732480

	* Finally, familiarise yourself with the Japanese mobile alert tones:
		* https://youtu.be/mlzGigIUgbY?t=42 - NHK’s early warning chime
		* https://youtu.be/mlzGigIUgbY?t=105 - This “whoop! whoop!” noise is the one on most people’s phones, and is worth being aware of
* In the *extremely* unlikely event that one really kicks off, move into the middle of the room, and take cover under anything sturdy - protecting your head is the main thing.
	* Otherwise, do as everyone else does, including moving inland if necessary

# 🚄 Transit

>[!info]
>It's japan, ffs.
>
>What you should really be worried about is every other transit system on the planet feeling inferior once you've spent enough time here.

*Seriously though:*

* Narita Airport connections:
	* Limited Expresses:
		* JR operate the N’EX, which goes direct to Yokohama
			* Special fare for foreigners if bought before flying is ¥4,070 return (usually ¥8,740)
		* Keisei operate the Skyliner, which takes you into Ueno, but is substantially faster
			* Special fare for foreigners if bought before flying is ¥4,480
	* Ordinary train:
		* Just use Suica via any reasonable route on either JR or Keisei
* **You may want to consider a Rail Pass**
	* Rail passes (at least the JR ones) include the N’EX, so long as their validity also covers it
	* They also include Shinkansen in their validity area
	* https://www.jreast.co.jp/multi/en/pass/
		* For example, ¥20,000 buys you 5 days of unlimited use in the entirety of Eastern Japan
		* ¥10,180 for all of Tokyo for 3 days
	* The downside is you have to use a paper ticket, the horror :(
* Remember, transit fares are distance and carrier based; you start a new fare when changing operator, so minimising changes of operator is key to keeping costs down
* Classes of Train:
	* Local, Rapid, Express, and Limited Express are the main ones, in order of stop frequency
		* On JR, **use great caution with Limited Express**, as these can require a supplement ticket
		* On most other operators this just means it’s an extremely limited stop service
	* Local means it stops at literally every station, so if in great doubt, just get on a Local
	* Super Express is reserved for the Shinkansen, which also requires a supplement ticket
		* Some trains also require seat reservations
* Green Car:
	* Only applicable on JR for longer-distance services (like those between Yokohama and Tokyo), not really a thing on other operators
	* You’ll figure this one out, ask if you’re unsure - probably only need to consider using it at peak times
* **ALWAYS queue for trains** at (usually denoted) boarding points
	* It’s ***never*** a free-for-all like London
* One last piece of advice: **never, under any circumstances, find yourself in a situation where you need to use the Yamanote or other major route in the morning rush hour**
	* **unless you have a kink for getting crushed to death** after waiting 20 minutes in the queue
	* you know [all](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FsAA0xHXbXs) [those](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ldx7RBWOCxs) [videos](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E7kor5nHtZQ) on youtube of people getting pushed into trains by station staff? yeah, those were on the Yamanote, Tokyo Metro, or the Osaka Loop Line (OLL). just delay your journey a bit, please, it's not fun.

# 🙇  Pleasantries & Customs
* **The important thing to remember: foreigners get a lot more slack on all of this stuff!**
* The general rule if in doubt is to pick the polite / respectful option and you’ll be fine!
* In professional contexts, **ALWAYS have business cards ready to go**
	* **And somewhere nice to put ones you receive - NOT your pocket!**
	* The general procedure is thus:
		* one person gives their business card with both hands, wording correctly oriented to recipient
		* recipient takes with both hands while thanking, studies intently (wow, these are definitely some words!)
		* recipient then puts card into nice, fancy business card holder, and reciprocates in turn
	* Business cards are seen as extensions of the self, so you should treat them with the same respect as the person that's giving it to you. This means no folding, no crumpled edges, no getting them wet, etc. *Have a business card holder ready to go.*
* The whole bowing thing:
	* A gentle bow as you’re greeting / saying goodbye to someone is generally appreciated
	* Deep bows are reserved for apologies, hopefully you won’t fuck up that bad though
* Shoes *always* come off when entering someone’s house.
	* This is why the Japanese love their slippers
	* Never, under any circumstances (unless you’ve explicitly been told it’s okay), wear shoes on tatami mat flooring (kinda looks like straw)
* *Please* don’t act confused, try to reciprocate or anything like that when you walk into a store and get *irasshaimase!* yelled at you, it’s a greeting used by literally every shop keeper - doing any of the aforementioned makes you stick out like a sore thumb, and you'll make everyone very confused. Just carry on with where you were heading like nothing even happened.
* Escalators: **stand on the left in Tokyo** and basically everywhere else, **unless in Osaka where you stand on the right** for some reason
* Photos / videos: Don’t take pictures of people unless you have their consent (*especially* indoors).
	* **Upskirt picture taking is still a major problem**, especially in younger parts of cities (e.g Akihabara). Keep an eye out, and report it to police / station staff if you notice it happening (it's a criminal offence since 2023, called "Satsueizai" (撮影罪) or "Tosatsu" (盗撮).)
