A workshop-based café that holds events on the first Saturday of each month. Each session lasts three hours, 10-1.

Much like our UK café, they focus on reducing waste/promoting re-use - customers stay with the item and are shown how it’s being repaired. They also offer workshops and training on various skills, including fabric repair - one of the fabric repairers is planning on running a zips workshop next month, which apparently is in high demand. (The other café in Wuppertal (there are two!) also runs Linux workshops of various technical skill levels on a routine basis.)

They also struggle with knowing how long to spend on an item before taking it home/deciding on an alternative resolution. We both agreed that these things sometimes just take time, and trying to put a specific time limit on a repair session invites inevitable over-run and added stress for all parties.

The physical layout of the café is like a long corridor with a couple of rooms to the side. The Sewing/Fabrics team is in one of these rooms. I asked about the cohesion between teams, whether having people tucked away made any difference - they empirically confirmed that they don’t have an issue with it (even the sewing team members I spoke to).

Front desk usually sends the visitor straight to a repair area, but they also know the specific skills and expertise of certain repairers (so might walk them straight through to a repairer). I mentioned that we have a two-step process (where our co-ordinator finds the right repairer for the task), which they were interested in hearing about. There are no pre-registrations, walk-in only.

There’s a small café area serving fresh coffee and tea by the entrance, with light bakery items, at cost price. Everyone is asked whether they’d like a drink immediately after registration even if they’re going straight to repair.

There’s also a small shop area just after registration with some fabrics, clothes, repair items, and bric-a-brac, as well as some children’s clothes made from scratch (think like a mini charity shop). These are sold at a minor profit in aid of the café.

They’re very strict on calling people that visit the café **_visitors_, never _customers_** - again for legal reasons, and also because the latter implies that there’s a specific contract/expectation of payment. While they solicit donations, that is absolutely never the focus, and they routinely have people that don’t donate - which they don’t see as a problem - although we agreed it does become one if someone keeps turning up with things and not contributing.

Their Repair Form isn’t much dissimilar to ours - though they also acquire addresses. All information is used exclusively for statistical purposes, and there’s a note on the form that they follow GDPR regulations (yup, they’re German alright). I will try to get a proper translation of the form from the friend I’m staying with in due course. The form also notes who primarily completed the repair - the visitor with help, the helper with support from the visitor, or just the helper on their own. Data entry is completed after the event. (Maybe we could ask them nicely for some statistics?)

Electrical testing to German standards is carried out before and after repair. If a device fails testing, the customer is informed, and if they wish to take it home, they must sign on the repair sheet that they have been informed that it must not be used under any circumstances. The Repair form specifically requires the Electrical Tester to tick the box to confirm testing has been completed (each authorised tester has their own box).

Repairs requiring spare parts (such as the coffee machine I saw that needed a new plunger/sealing ring) are purchased by the customer and brought to the café, except in exceptional cases (for similar reasons to us, they prefer not to have to worry about moving money).

In conclusion: The most striking thing to me was how similar it was to our own operation, just in a fixed, larger workshop space. This is probably a good sign that we’re doing at least something right!

___
My thanks to the entire team there, who were all extremely friendly and helpful. I have, of course, extended an invitation to come and visit our operation at some point.
